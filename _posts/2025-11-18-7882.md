# Understanding 35mm Film Scanner Resolution: What It Really Means and Why It Matters
## **Understanding Film Scanner Resolution: What It Really Means and Why It Matters**

One of the decisions to face when adopting a digital workflow for negative scanning is exactly how to scan.   In this post I’m focusing on a single question-one that everyone seems to have an opinion about: how much resolution do you actually need?

It is a simple topic until you start thinkng about how billboard artwork was created using old timey film cameras.  We aren’t going to go into that rabbit hole. Instead this post is aimed at the consumer (maybe prosumer too) photographer who’s returning to film after years of digital photography, or the new comer just arriving on the scene.

My own voyage comes out of shooting film (mostly Kodachrome) back in the day. When digital cameras emerged, I was excited to eliminate the cost and hassle of dealing with K64.  After years of digital only photography I began to feel a little stagnate. I needed a change.  Lucky that happened at about the same time that film photography began making a resurgence. 

I gravitated toward black and white film photography (even in digital I tend to work in black and white). I started shooting black and white film in my newly purchased Olympus XA but soon grew disatisfied with the lack of control over the negatives when sending them to labs. Asking for higher quality scans and special developing would quickly become expensive. That is when I began developing and scanning at home. I wanted to have complete artistic control over the images from start to finish.

## **Discovering the Hybrid Workflow**

Once I decided to develop and scan film myself, I tooled up the equipment and materials. Developing is straightforward; not much had changed since back in the day. But the hybrid workflow-analog capture with digital process had changed dramatically.

High-resolution, high-quality film scanning is available to  home photographers for not too much money. And the hybrid workflow enables printing at sizes that once required gigantic darkroom enlargers.  I kept one goal front and center: big prints.  Bigger than A4 (8-1/2″ X 11″). As big as I had room to accommodate and afford.

With this framework, I began looking for a film scanner. 

I had some particular requirements (detailed in another post), but at the top of the list was the highest true resolution I could afford.  What I’ve learned is that scanners come in all sorts of resolutions, some comically low and suitable only for phone viewing and others very high resolution and wildly expensive-and only available used.  Not for me, I want something in middle ground. 

One of the challenges in scanner evaluation is understanding what the scanning resolution means in terms of the image. Scanners are sold as have a certain ppi (pixel per inch) resolution. PPI relates to several things at once:

- Image size

- File size

- Maximum print sizes

- True captured detail

I couldn’t find any clear explanations tying these together. so I worked it out myself.  This post is my attempt to contextualise scanner resolution in practical meaningful terms.

This post focuses on 16-bit greyscale scanning of 35mm black-and-white film, because that is what I do.  To keep things sane I’m skipping deeper engineering topics that don’t matter for most photographers.  Keep this one thing in mind: more pixels are generally a good thing-*as long as they’re real.*

Scanner marketing claims are (mostly) fiction.

Like many tech categories, scanner marketing tends to dramatically exaggerate the specifications-especially resolution. Let’s take the the scanner I purchased, the Reflecta RPS 10M as an example.

It is marketed as a 10,000 pixel per inch (ppi) scanner.  

Actual tested resolution? 4300 ppi.

What happens if you to select 10,000 ppi as a scan resolution?  The scanner simply interpolates the image – creating pixels that were never captured on the negative.  These invented pixels make the files larger but don’t add real detail.

Here’s why that matters

- Interpolated pixels may or may not align with your artistic intent.

- File sizes balloon dramatically.

**Why Actual Resolution Matters**

Actual tested resolution, not the number on the box, is what determines your real image quality. For the Reflecta RPS 10M, independent tests confirm 4300 PPI of true optical resolution. That’s excellent for a modern, readily available prosumer scanner, and enough to produce A2-sized prints (16.5″ × 23.4″) without upscaling in software.

## **Other scanning approaches and resolution**

**Camera Copy Stand**

Another popular approach is using a digital camera on a copy stand. With proper setup; macro lens, film holder, lighting, you can achieve extremely high-quality results. It takes more space and more pieces, but it works.

A 60 MP full-frame camera effectively captures a 35mm film negative at the equivalent of 6695 PPI. That supports A1 prints (23.4″ × 33.1″) straight from the file. This is far beyond the capabilities of any current consumer scanner.

**Flatbed Scanners**

Epson V-series flatbeds are widely used for film scanning. They’re marketed at 4800–6400 PPI, but real-world tests show more like 2300–2500 PPI; about the equivalent of a 7 MP full-frame sensor–enough pixels for screen use or perhaps A4 prints, but not much more.

A 4300 PPI scan (like the Reflecta’s true capability) is roughly equal to a 24 MP full-frame camera; the same as my Leica M10-P. A 5000 PPI scan is equivalent to ~33 MP. A 60 MP camera on a copy stand surpasses any consumer scanner by a wide margin.

## **What Resolution Do You Need?**

**For large physical prints**

You’ll want the highest true resolution you can afford. Printers like the Epson P900 or Canon Pro-1100 benefit from detailed input files. Lightroom’s “Super Resolution” can upscale images by doubling linear resolution, but whether that’s acceptable is up to you and your artistic goals.

**For screen-only work**

Almost any scanner, even a flatbed, will suffice.

**For archival or future-proofing and bigger than A4 prints**

A dedicated film scanner makes the most sense.  Scanning at the highest *true* optical resolution of your scanner is a sensible approach, storage permitting.

The naive assumption that “more is better” isn’t entirely wrong; it just needs the important caveat: more real pixels are better. Not invented ones.

Scan at the highest *authentic* resolution your equipment offers, and choose a workflow that fits your artistic goals, budget, and available space.
