# When the Scanning Workflow Breaks: Troubleshooting Banding on the Reflecta RPS 10M
Since returning to film photography, my equipment inventory has grown significantly. For me, *film means black and white*—that’s my preference, and it’s where I feel the most control. With black and white, you can manage the entire process from exposure to development, to scanning, to editing in Lightroom or Photoshop, and finally to printing. In the business world we used to call this **vertical integration**. Everything under one roof.

### **Why Scanning Matters So Much**

Because I don’t have a darkroom, nor is there space for one in this small apartment, traditional enlarging isn’t an option. Getting my negatives into the digital realm is therefore a **critical step** in the workflow. High-quality scanning is the bridge between analog capture and digital control.

### **The Reflecta RPS 10M: My “Set It and Forget It” Scanner**

I picked up a **Reflecta RPS 10M** from Scandig.de along with **SilverFast 9 Ai**. One of the major reasons I chose this scanner was its ability to handle **entire 35mm rolls automatically**, at high resolution, with zero intervention. Load the roll, press go, walk away. Typically I get 38 frames per roll, and the Reflecta processes them without complaint.

For a couple of months, this workflow was rock-solid: shoot, develop, scan, edit. A few times I noticed some faint horizontal banding, but the affected frames weren’t keepers so I let it slide. (A quick aside: *never waste time editing images that aren’t keepers.*)

### **When the Problem Became Impossible to Ignore**

Then came the most recent batch of scans. Several frames showed clear horizontal banding, and this time one of the affected images was actually interesting, possibly a keeper, if I could resolve the banding and pull a bit more range out of the scan.

And that’s where the entrance to the **scanner troubleshooting rabbit hole** opened up.

## **Chasing the Banding Ghost: When Software Becomes the Problem**

With the negative strip loaded back into the Reflecta, I ran a pre-scan and started fiddling with adjustments to see if I could coax the image into usable shape. Improving the tonal range was easy enough, but the banding was stubborn—it refused to budge.

### **First Round of Support: Not Very Helpful**

I reached out to the retailer where I bought the scanner and SilverFast. Their help amounted to little more than suggesting I contact Reflecta and maybe check for a firmware update that *might* fix the issue. Not exactly confidence-inspiring.

### **Reflecta’s Response: Not Our Problem (Yet)**

So I contacted Reflecta directly and sent them sample scans. Everything was fine until they realized I wasn’t using **CyberViewX**, the scanning software that ships with the machine. At that point they politely (but firmly) said they could only help if the scans were made using CyberViewX.

Fair enough.  I had never even tried their software. Out of curiosity, I set it up and rescanned the exact same frame.

**No banding. None. Clean as can be.**

That was the first real clue. The hardware wasn’t the culprit. This was looking more and more like a **SilverFast** issue. And now things were getting interesting.

### **SilverFast Support: Hurry Up and Wait**

Next stop: SilverFast’s online support form. I quickly noticed that if you want timely help, you’re expected to pay for it. They offer two support plans:

- **60€/year** — 6-hour email response + phone number

- **240€/year (Premium)** — 4-hour email response + priority phone line

I’m not holding my breath waiting for a response on the free tier.

### **The Overexposure Factor**

I’m fully aware the negative is **grossly overexposed**—that part is on me. It was a surprise to discover that all eight images in that series were similarly cooked. Exposure misses happen, hopefully not often, but they do happen.

On properly exposed negatives I’ve **never** seen banding using this scanner/software combination. So this feels like an edge case where SilverFast is struggling in ways CyberViewX isn’t.

### **Why I’m Even Bothering**

If the images weren’t interesting, I’d have dropped this days ago. Under normal circumstances, these frames would have gone straight into the “hopeless” pile back in the contact sheet era—no second chances.

But this is the double-edged sword of a digital workflow.
It gives us tools to rescue borderline frames…
…and it tempts us to spend hours **monkeying with marginal images** instead of shooting, editing, or printing the good ones.

Still, there’s something in this series I want to salvage. So down the rabbit hole I go.
