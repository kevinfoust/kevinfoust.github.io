# SilverFast9 Ai &amp; Reflecta 10M Manual Focus
## When the Scanner Misses Focus: A Tri-X Lesson from the Camino

We’d been walking the Camino for a couple of weeks and I carried a simple setup: a Leica M3, a 35mm Summicron, and a stash of Tri-X. Back home, I developed the film and batch-scanned all five rolls on my Reflecta RPS 10M using SilverFast 9 Ai. Once everything landed in Lightroom, a couple of the keepers looked subtly out of focus.

That made no sense.

Daylight Tri-X wants to be stopped down, and with a 35mm Summicron I was living at f/8 most of the time—plenty of depth of field. And focusing the M3 is second nature for me; I’m not bragging, it’s just a camera I’ve used long enough to trust. The negatives themselves looked sharp enough. Yet the A4 print? Clearly soft.

This raised an unsettling question:
**Can a film scanner miss focus?**
If modern digital cameras can, why not a desktop scanner?

### Digging into SilverFast’s Focus Tools

I had never bothered with the manual focus options in SilverFast because everything had looked fine up to this point. But after [some reading on the SilverFast site](https://www.silverfast.com/about-silverfast-why-scanning-basics-of-scanning/why-silverfast/silverfast-feature-highlights/focus-control-scan-adjust-focus-manually-thick-originals/), I learned how to manually adjust focus on the Reflecta.

I reloaded the negative, ran a pre-scan, and used the manual focus control for the first time. The improvement was immediate and obvious.

A quick note: pre-scan speed matters when you’re making iterative adjustments. On the Reflecta RPS 10M, it’s quick enough, under a minute, so experimenting is painless.

### “Better, Worse, or the Same?”

The manual focus process feels a lot like an eye exam. You’re essentially comparing tiny differences:
*Better? Worse? Or the same?*

To make that comparison easier, I took screenshots of the focus preview window as I went. Having those side-by-side made the subtle shifts far easier to judge.

### The Results

After dialing in the best focus, I rescanned the negative at full resolution (4300 ppi actual). The new scan produced a noticeably sharper A4 print. It confirmed what I suspected right from the start: the original softness wasn’t my focusing with the M3—it was the scanner.

**Takeaway:**
Even if your workflow has been consistent and reliable, it’s worth learning your scanner’s manual focus tools. Once in a while, they make all the difference.

Manual Focus in SilverFast: A Quick Look at the Process

Below are screenshots of the SilverFast focus preview dialog. The process is straightforward and, for a photographer, surprisingly intuitive. You simply zoom in on the area of the frame that matters most, then adjust the blue slider to shift the position of the scanner’s lens. Hit **UPDATE**, let the preview refresh, and evaluate the result.

SilverFast’s **Before/After** windows make it easy to compare changes side by side, turning the whole focusing exercise into a quick and efficient part of the scanning workflow.
