# Scanners and Software: Building a Home Film-Scanning Workflow
# I Need a 35mm Film Scanner

As I began my voyage adding film back into my photography the level of control didn’t satisfy me. There is a lab a couple of blocks from home and they could develop and scan B&W film but the results are decidedly low-fi. At first I kind of liked that low-fi look; until I had some photos that needed a little correction here and there. The same low-fi .jpgs from the lab just weren’t going to cut it. At home development is just one piece of the workflow and pretty directly solved.

On the finished image side, there is no place for an enlarger in our apartment. Even if there were, I’m not so sure that I’d head down that path. My workflow is ultimately digital. Scanning at home to get the analog film into my workflow becomes a critical piece of the workflow puzzle...

If you’re planning to print big, *A2 big*, higher resolution is non-negotiable.

Since I shoot five or more rolls of film a month, outsourcing drains money fast. Around here a develop-and-scan package for 36 black-and-white frames runs about **25**€ for low-fi JPEGs. Higher-quality scans jump to around **2€ per frame** or MORE.  And I still wouldn’t get the level of control I want as both an artist and an engineer.

So I decided to take over the entire process.

Yes, the trade-off is capital cost versus paying labs over time; but I get to choose. And there’s another benefit: I actually *learn* the scanning craft instead of handing it off.

Now I’m what we used to call, back in my professional life, **“vertically integrated.”**
Shoot → develop → scan → process → print.
All under my own roof. I love it.

# **What I Wanted in a Scanner**

- **High resolution**, so BIG prints (A2) are easy. Lightroom and Photoshop’s “Super Resolution” might help too—I haven’t tested them yet.

- **Batch scanning of entire rolls.** No babysitting required.

- **Color calibration (later on)** so I’m not wasting hours correcting color in Photoshop. I already hate color correction with digital files; film in mixed light? Hard pass.

# **Scanner + Software = Workflow**

The biggest revelation: the hardware is one thing, the software is another. Your results depend on both.

Different software packages produce widely different scan times on the same scanner. For example:

- **SilverFast**: ~5 minutes per frame

- **CyberView**: ~8 minutes per frame (same frame, similar quality)

For batch work, this isn't catastrophic. For manual scanning, it matters *a lot*.

Other differences across scanning software include output formats, manual controls, stability, and support. If you're researching **how to scan film at home**, this part matters as much as the hardware.

One of the biggest lessons: the scanner is one thing—**the software is another**.

# **Resolution Reality Check**

If you're researching **film scanner reviews**, you’ll run into the usual tech marketing inflation.

My scanner, the **Reflecta RPS 10M**, is advertised as a **10,000 ppi scanner**. Independent tests show real optical resolution is closer to **4,300 ppi**.

This has huge implications for file size:

- **10,000 ppi TIFF**: ~200 MB

- **4,300 ppi TIFF**: ~60 MB

Multiply that by a few rolls and suddenly you're shopping for more storage.

# **Connectivity Matters** (More Than You Think)

**Connectivity Matters (More Than You Think)**

The Reflecta uses the old square **USB 2.0** connector. My iMac only has **USB-C**.  A direct USB-C → USB-2.0 cable technically worked but caused software issues. The fix:

iMac → **USB hub** (with USB 2.0 logic) → scanner

Once I switched to the hub, everything became reliable. If you’re considering a **35mm film scanner** today, expect legacy connectivity.  Even with brand new scanners!

# 35mm Film **Scanner Options (2025)**

Reflecta, Pacific Scanning (PrimeFilm), and Plustek all make capable film scanners. Slide shooters can get magazine-fed models; others rely on holders and semi-automatic workflows.

## **Reflecta**

[https://reflecta.de/en/23-computer-required](https://reflecta.de/en/23-computer-required)

I use the **Reflecta RPS 10M** (model before the current 10S). It’s the highest-resolution 35mm scanner currently available new. The differences between the 10M and 10S seem minimal.

Key features:

- Scans complete 35mm rolls in batch mode

- Handles mounted slides

- Focus ability—important because film isn’t always perfectly flat

I bought mine from **ScanDig**:
[https://www.scandig.de](https://www.scandig.de/)

They speak excellent English and really know the hardware. Their review site is the most technically detailed I’ve found:
[https://www.filmscanner.info/FilmscannerTestberichte.html](https://www.filmscanner.info/FilmscannerTestberichte.html)

## **Pacific Scanning**

[https://www.scanace.com/product/film-scanners](https://www.scanace.com/product/film-scanners)
The **PrimeFilm XA Plus** appears to be the same as Reflecta 10M/10S.

## **Plustek**

[https://plustek.com/us/products/index.php?products=film-photo-scanners](https://plustek.com/us/products/index.php?products=film-photo-scanners)

Solid lineup, including medium-format scanners, but **no unattended 36-frame roll scanning**—a dealbreaker for me.

## **Epson Flatbeds**

Familiar, versatile, and great for medium format like 6x4.5, 6x9 and large formats like 4×5 or 8×10. But for 35mm film they’re:

- low resolution (2300 ppi good for A4 without heroics)

- slower

- more hands-on

They *are* useful if you plan to scan documents, books, or prints—is that really important to you: flatbed scanners are a big compromise when it comes to scanning 35mm film.

## **The Used High-End Market**

There are legendary scanners out there—Nikon and Hasselblad in particular—but they’re only available used and often cost:

- **Nikon:** ~1,500-2,000 USD

- **Hasselblad:** Alot more

Amazing gear, but pricey and aging.

# Film **Scanning Software**

There are three main third-party scanning software options. Some OEM tools exist (e.g., Epson’s), but I haven’t used them.

## **SilverFast 9 Ai**

[https://www.silverfast.com](https://www.silverfast.com)

My current favorite. Excellent fidelity, deep control, and good automation for well-exposed frames. The learning curve is real, but worth it.

**Cost:** ~250€
**Support:** Paid support available. Free support… slow/non-existent (still waiting on a response after weeks).

Ideal for users who want maximum image quality, speed and personal control.

## **VueScan**

[https://www.hamrick.com](https://www.hamrick.com)

Highly automated and easy to use. Trial version watermarks your scans. Great for normal exposures; less ideal for difficult negatives—which I seem to specialize in.

**Pricing:** complicated
**Support:** unclear

## **CyberViewX**

OEM software for many scanners, including the Reflecta.

- Easy to use

- Limited manual controls

- Free

- Good for beginners

Documentation mostly comes from Pacific Scanning.

**Support:** mostly through Pacific Scanning’s documentation.

# **The “Copy Stand” Option**

Some people photograph their negatives with a digital camera. While I *could* use my Leica M10-P and macro adapter, the whole setup—copy stand, lights, anti-reflection work, film holders, inversion software—looks like a rabbit hole.

I even considered buying a dedicated digital camera for it, but I knew I’d never use it for anything else.

Plus, the entire process is **fully manual**.

And honestly? I just prefer dedicated tools. They work better.

Have you ever bought a drill that also works as a saw?
